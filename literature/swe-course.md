# Lecture 1 — What This Course Is Actually About

## What was said

Software engineering as a discipline distinct from programming. Programming is a skill, software engineering is the practice of building systems that other people — including future-you — can understand, change, and trust. The course will be evaluated not just on the working product but on the *traceability* of the engineering decisions behind it.

The two repositories assigned in week one (the product repo and the PKM repo) are not parallel deliverables. The PKM is the substrate; the product is what grows out of it.

## What I think about it

This is the first time a course has been explicit about the meta-skill instead of the technical skill. I have built plenty of software, and at work I have shipped real features for two and a half years, but I have never been required to *trace my own reasoning*. The Git-as-thinking-history idea is the part I want to take most seriously — partly for the course, partly because as a Team Lead I now owe my team a paper trail of why we are doing what we are doing.

The risk I see: the structure will collapse the moment work gets busy. The first two weeks will be easy to maintain. Week six, when TaskMaster is mid-implementation and I have a sprint deadline at ClarityLabs, is when this will be tested.

## What I will do

- Treat the PKM as non-optional infrastructure, not an extra. If I am committing code to TaskMaster but not committing notes to PKM, something is wrong.
- The Sunday review is the linchpin. Skip it twice in a row and the system is dead.
